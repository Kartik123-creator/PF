I've built marketing sites and listing platforms where organic traffic was the
whole business model. The lesson: content teams can't out-write a slow,
badly-rendered site. The engineering half of SEO is unglamorous, measurable,
and almost always neglected.

## Rendering strategy is decision #1

Google can execute JavaScript, but rendering budget is real and crawl-to-index
delays on client-rendered pages are noticeable. My defaults in Next.js:

- **Static generation** for anything whose content doesn't depend on the viewer —
  landing pages, listings, blog posts. It's the fastest thing you can serve and
  crawlers love it.
- **Server rendering** only when the page genuinely varies per request.
- **Client rendering** for what's behind a login, where SEO is irrelevant anyway.

If a page matters for search and ships as a client-side spinner, that's the bug
to fix before anything else.

## Core Web Vitals: fix the usual three suspects

Every site I've audited had the same culprits:

1. **LCP** — the hero image. Use `next/image` with `priority`, size it properly,
   and serve modern formats. Nothing else matters until this is right.
2. **CLS** — anything that loads without reserved space. Set explicit dimensions
   on images, embeds and ad slots. Fonts: `font-display: swap` plus a matched
   fallback metric.
3. **INP** — third-party scripts. Audit what marketing added two years ago.
   Half of it is usually dead weight.

## Structured data is free real estate

Search engines reward pages that explain themselves:

```tsx
<script type="application/ld+json">
  {JSON.stringify({
    "@context": "https://schema.org",
    "@type": "Product",
    name: listing.title,
    offers: { "@type": "Offer", price: listing.price },
  })}
</script>
```

On listing-heavy sites, valid structured data is the difference between a plain
blue link and a rich result. It's an afternoon of work per page type.

## The boring checklist that compounds

None of these are exciting; all of them are why two equally good sites rank differently:

- One `h1` per page, with a heading tree that doesn't skip levels
- Canonical URLs on every page — duplicate-content penalties are self-inflicted
- An accurate `sitemap.xml` with real `lastModified` dates
- Descriptive, unique titles and meta descriptions per route
- Internal links with meaningful anchor text, not "click here"

Rankings move slowly, which is exactly why teams skip this work — and exactly
why doing it puts you ahead of the ones who did.
