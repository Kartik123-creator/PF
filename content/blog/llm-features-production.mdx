Adding an LLM call to a product takes an afternoon. Making users trust the
result enough to come back is the actual project. After integrating AI features
into production apps, my mental model has settled: **treat the model as a
brilliant intern — never let it answer unsupervised about things it didn't read.**

## Ground every answer in your own content

The single biggest trust-killer is the model confidently inventing details about
*your* product. The fix isn't a smarter model; it's narrower input:

```js
const answer = await llm.complete({
  system: `Answer ONLY from the provided context.
           If the context doesn't cover it, say so and offer the human channel.`,
  context: await retrieveRelevantDocs(userQuestion),
  question: userQuestion,
});
```

"I don't know, but here's how to reach a human" reads as honest.
A fabricated refund policy reads as a lawsuit.

## Design the failure path first

LLM calls fail in ways normal APIs don't: timeouts, rate limits, and the special
one — a response that arrives fine but is *wrong-shaped*. Before shipping, decide:

- What renders when the call times out? (A retry button, not a spinner forever.)
- What happens when output fails validation? Re-ask once with the error included,
  then fall back to a non-AI path.
- Where's the human handoff? Every AI conversation needs an exit that
  doesn't feel like a dead end.

## Stream, because perceived speed is speed

A four-second wait followed by a wall of text feels broken. The same four seconds
spent watching the answer type itself feels alive. Streaming is mostly plumbing —
server-sent events or a socket — and it's the cheapest UX upgrade in the whole stack.

The detail people forget: **make streamed output interruptible.** Users who can
see the answer going wrong want a stop button, not a forced wait.

## Log prompts like you log payments

When a user reports "the AI said something weird," you need the exact prompt,
context, model version and response — or you're debugging fog. Structured logs
of every AI interaction turned vague complaints into reproducible bugs, and
they doubled as the dataset for improving prompts later.

## The quiet conclusion

Users don't want "AI features." They want their problem solved with less effort,
and they'll forgive a lot if the feature is honest about its limits. Ground it,
plan the failures, stream the output, log everything — that's the unglamorous
80% that the demo never shows.
